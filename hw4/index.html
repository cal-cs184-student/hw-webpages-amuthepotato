Part 1: Masses and springs

This part of the assignment asked us to implement a basic cloth simulation model using a grid of point masses connected by springs. We started by constructing a uniformly spaced grid of point masses based on the cloth’s width, height, and resolution, making sure to store them in row-major order. Depending on whether the cloth is oriented horizontally or vertically, we assigned positions in either the xz-plane or xy-plane (with a slight random offset in the z-direction for vertical cloth). We also marked certain masses as pinned according to the provided pinned indices. After creating all the point masses, we then established spring connections between them to enforce structural, shearing, and bending constraints. These springs connect neighboring masses in specific patterns (adjacent, diagonal, and two units apart) and are stored for use in the simulation. Finally, we verifyed our implementation visually by rendering the cloth and toggling different constraint types to ensure the correct springs are being applied.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

In this part, we extended the cloth model by simulating its motion over time using physical forces and numerical integration. We computed the total forces acting on each point mass by combining external forces (like gravity) with spring forces derived from Hooke’s Law, accounting for structural, shearing, and bending constraints. Using these forces, we updated each point mass’s position via Verlet integration, which estimates motion based on current and previous positions while incorporating a damping factor to simulate energy loss. Finally, we enforced deformation constraints to prevent springs from stretching beyond a reasonable limit, ensuring the cloth behaves in a stable and realistic manner during simulation.

Spring Constant

The spring constant ks controls how stiff the cloth is by determining how strongly the springs resist deformation. With a very low ks, the cloth behaves loosely and appears extremely flexible, often stretching significantly and sagging under gravity before slowly settling into a shape with exaggerated deformations. In contrast, a high ks makes the cloth much stiffer, causing it to better preserve its original shape and resist stretching. As a result, the cloth falls more rigidly and reaches a stable configuration more quickly, with minimal visible deformation between neighboring point masses.

Density

Density affects the mass of each point mass in the cloth, which in turn influences how the cloth responds to forces like gravity. With a higher density, the cloth becomes heavier, causing it to fall more quickly and stretch the springs more due to the increased force. This can lead to more pronounced sagging and tension in the cloth before it stabilizes. Conversely, a lower density results in a lighter cloth that falls more slowly and exhibits less dramatic stretching, often appearing more delicate and responsive to smaller forces.

Damping

Damping controls how much energy is lost over time in the simulation, effectively reducing the cloth’s motion as it evolves. With low damping, the cloth retains more of its kinetic energy, leading to prolonged oscillations and visible bouncing before coming to rest. In the screenshot, the cloth is still in motion. With high damping, motion is quickly reduced, and the cloth settles into its final position much faster with minimal oscillation. However, excessive damping can make the cloth appear unnaturally sluggish or overdamped, removing realistic secondary motion.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

To handle collisions with spheres, we implemented a method that checks whether a point mass lies inside or intersects the sphere by comparing its distance from the sphere’s center to the sphere’s radius. If the point mass is inside the sphere, we computed the direction vector from the sphere’s origin to the point mass and normalized it to find the point on the sphere’s surface where the mass should lie (the tangent point). We then calculated a correction vector from the point mass’s previous position (last_position) to this tangent point. The point mass’s position is updated by applying this correction, scaled by a factor of (1−f) to account for friction. This ensures that the cloth doesn't penetrate the sphere and instead slides naturally along its surface while losing some energy due to friction. Additionally, we updated the simulation loop to check every point mass against all collision objects in the scene at each time step.

For plane collisions, we implemented a method to detect whether a point mass crosses the plane between time steps by checking if its current position lies on a different side of the plane than its previous position. When such a crossing is detected, we computed the tangent point where the point mass would have intersected the plane along its trajectory. We then calculated a correction vector that moves the point mass back to a position slightly above the plane, using a small offset (SURFACE_OFFSET) to prevent numerical instability or repeated collisions. This correction is applied to the point mass’s previous position and scaled by (1−f) to incorporate friction. As a result, the cloth settles naturally on the plane surface without clipping through it, and friction helps dampen its motion over time.

When simulating the cloth draping over a sphere, the spring constant ks significantly affects how the cloth conforms to the surface. With a low ks value such as 500, the cloth is very flexible and stretches easily, causing it to drape closely over the sphere with pronounced sagging and deeper folds. At the default ks = 5000, the cloth strikes a balance between flexibility and stiffness, producing realistic folds while still maintaining some structural integrity. With a high ks value such as 50000, the cloth becomes much stiffer and resists deformation, resulting in a looser fit over the sphere with fewer and less pronounced folds. Instead of wrapping tightly around the sphere, the cloth maintains a more rigid shape.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

We implemented self-collision detection using spatial hashing. Rather than checking every pair of point masses, we build a hash table at each timestep that maps a float key to a vector<PointMass *>, where each key uniquely identifies a 3D box volume in the scene and the vector contains all point masses currently inside that box.

The hash function partitions 3D space into boxes of dimensions w, h, t, where w = 3 * width / num_width_points, h = 3 * height / num_height_points, and t = max(w, h). Each point mass's position is truncated to its containing box using floor, and the three box indices are combined into a unique float using large prime multipliers to minimize collisions.

Self-collision is then handled by looking up each point mass's hash bucket to find nearby candidates. For each candidate closer than 2 * thickness, we compute a correction vector that would push the two point masses exactly 2 * thickness apart. The final correction applied to the point mass is the average of all pairwise corrections, divided by simulation_steps to keep position changes smooth across sub-steps. build_spatial_map() is rebuilt at every timestep so the buckets always reflect current positions.

Part 5: Shaders

A shader is a small, isolated program that runs on the GPU in parallel across many vertices or pixels at once, making real-time rendering feasible where CPU-based approaches like ray tracing are far too slow. The two shader types we use work together as a pipeline. The vertex shader runs first per vertex, transforming geometry from model space into screen space and passing per-vertex data like normals, UV coordinates, and tangents forward as varyings. The rasterizer then interpolates those varyings across each triangle face using barycentric coordinates. The fragment shader runs next per pixel, receiving those interpolated values and computing a final output color. The vertex shader handles where things are, and the fragment shader handles what they look like.

Blinn-Phong Shading

Blinn-Phong models surface lighting as the sum of three components. The ambient term is a small constant added everywhere, simulating indirect bounced light so nothing is ever completely black. The diffuse term models light scattering based on the angle between the surface normal and the light direction, so surfaces facing the light directly appear brighter, and the effect falls off with distance. The specular term produces the shiny highlight characteristic of glossy surfaces. I used ka = 0.1, kd = 0.8, ks = 0.5, p = 64, and Ia = vec3(1.0).

Texture Mapping

The vertex shader passes interpolated UV coordinates v_uv to the fragment shader, which samples the texture using texture(u_texture_1, v_uv) and outputs the result directly as the fragment color. The UV interpolation across each triangle face ensures the texture maps smoothly onto the mesh geometry.

Bump Mapping vs Displacement Mapping

Both bump mapping and displacement mapping use a height map texture to add surface detail, but they operate at different stages of the pipeline. Bump mapping only modifies surface normals in the fragment shader. By computing finite differences in the height map along u and v, we tilt the local normal to simulate the appearance of bumps without changing any geometry. This is computationally cheap and looks convincing at medium range, but the silhouette of the mesh remains perfectly smooth and there are no real shadows cast by the bumps.

Displacement mapping physically moves vertex positions in the vertex shader along the normal direction by h(u,v) * kh. This produces real geometric deformation and the mesh actually bumps outward. The tradeoff is that quality is entirely dependent on mesh resolution and displacement can only appear where vertices exist.

At -o 16 -a 16 (low resolution), bump mapping looks roughly the same as at high resolution since it works purely in the fragment shader and is independent of vertex count. Displacement mapping at low resolution, however, produces chunky, exaggerated spikes and there are too few vertices to faithfully represent the texture's detail, so each displaced vertex moves a large visible distance.

At -o 128 -a 128 (high resolution), bump mapping is again largely unchanged. Displacement mapping improves as the dense mesh captures fine detail from the height map, and the deformation looks smooth and consistent with the texture pattern.

Mirror Shader

The mirror shader computes the outgoing eye ray wo from the camera position to the fragment, reflects it across the surface normal to get the incoming direction wi, and samples the environment cubemap at wi. This simulates a perfect mirror material. The cloth and sphere appear to reflect the surrounding environment, with the reflection updating correctly as the view angle changes.
